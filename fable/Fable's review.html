01

Editorial opening

There is a genre of repository that pretends to be configuration and is actually an argument. This is one of them. Nominally, it is a copy-from catalog: eighteen language profiles, a shared mise command grammar, a manifest, and a folder of miniature fixture projects. Actually, it is a sustained position paper on what code should feel like — obvious, self-documenting, elegant through directness, in its own words (AGENTS.md) — written in the only medium where such claims can be checked: executable policy.

Three design commitments organize everything. First, one verb set for every ecosystem: install, fmt, lint, test, standards, standards:check, dispatched by file-marker detection so a polyglot repo answers to one grammar (Mise/config.toml). Second, copies must be provable: the manifest (standards.manifest.toml) declares byte-for-byte mirrors between each canonical template and its fixture, and a 613-line checker (scripts/check-standards-drift.py) enforces them — going as far as executing the dispatcher shell script against a fake mise shim to verify that every marker routes to the right profile. Third, time is an enemy: every tool is version-pinned, every fixture commits a mise.lock, and ecosystems that lack lockfiles get them invented — a hash-pinned PEP 751 lock for a game engine's linter, an awk-verified Alire lock for Ada, an immutable Git revision for Fortran's one dependency.

The repository names two audiences and means both. The primary voice is a senior systems engineer's taste — grug-brained by citation, allergic to ceremony. The secondary audience is explicitly non-human: agents should discover the intended workflow … make a narrow change, and prove it through deterministic commands without relying on tribal knowledge (shared/AGENTS.md). Read that way, the catalog is one of the more serious attempts I have seen to design an engineering environment where a language model is a first-class contributor — not by adding AI tooling, but by removing everything that requires being in the room: hidden state, floating versions, folklore commands, checks that only a veteran knows how to interpret.

What follows is an evaluation, not a work order. The catalog is opinionated enough to deserve opinions back: where its strictness protects and where it postures, where its uniformity clarifies and where it flattens, and what kind of engineering culture would actually thrive inside it. Every material claim cites a file path and carries a confidence label; scores are interpretive instruments, not measurements.

Its strongest convictions. That drift is the default state of copied things, and must be fought mechanically rather than by discipline. That version pins are a moral position: the root mise.lock resolves down to per-platform artifact URLs and checksums, and the Dagger runner starts from an image pinned by multi-arch digest (Dagger/dagger/src/index.ts). That the type checker is the most trustworthy reviewer available — Python welds the Any escape hatch shut, TypeScript abolishes truthiness, SPARK puts mathematical proof in the merge gate. And that documentation lies unless executed: the catalog’s own prose is formatted, linted, compiled, link-checked, and spell-checked by the same Markdown profile it distributes.

Its most interesting tensions. A repository whose first principle is complexity is the enemy… boring, strict, portable defaults over clever local machinery ships, as its crown jewels, a 235-line type-theory-literate Go analyzer, a 300-line shell classification engine, two awk programs that parse TOML by regex, and a drift checker with its own embedded test harness. The resolution it offers — visible in how thoroughly each machine is tested — is that boring means predictable, not small. Whether that resolution convinces you is the central question of this review.

Humans and agents. The catalog treats the two audiences asymmetrically: agents get determinism, humans get prose. Error messages are written in full sentences with remediation (Run mise run md:lock and commit the generated lockfile); READMEs argue rather than dictate; and every strict default comes with an explicit invitation to relax it (README.md, “Adoption Posture”). But the daily texture — warnings-as-errors everywhere, checksum verification, proof obligations — is unmistakably tuned for contributors (human or not) who prefer being stopped early to being surprised late.

The profiles that best represent the whole. Python is the reference implementation — every catalog principle has a working example there, and it alone earns containerized proof. Rust shows the philosophy at peace with its ecosystem; Go shows it at war with one; Zig is the control group proving the catalog can also choose nothing; and Markdown/MDX is the quiet flagship, the one standard the repository provably lives under every day.

04

Strictness, friction & coherence

The population hugs the diagonal: in this catalog, friction largely is the price of strictness, honestly paid. Three departures matter. Below the line sit Rust, Elixir, and Markdown — the coherence dividend, where strictness rides on tools the ecosystem already loves, so the toll feels smaller than the toll booth suggests. Above the line sits Kotlin, whose checksum verification ritual buys integrity at a friction cost exceeding even its considerable strictness — the one profile where the daily tax outruns the stated policy. The extremes are the catalog arguing with itself: Zig demonstrates how little machinery a cooperative compiler requires; SPARK demonstrates how much a formal-methods niche will tolerate. That both live comfortably in one repository is the strongest evidence that the six-verb grammar standardizes names, not natures — and a caution against reading the x-axis as a virtue scale. Maximal strictness is a position, and half this chart is a list of reasons not to take it.

05

Standards architecture map

The diagram’s point is the direction of the arrows. Templates flow down into fixtures and out into projects, but authority flows through the manifest: nothing counts as a profile unless standards.manifest.toml says so, and the drift checker holds every declared mirror to byte identity — then goes further and interrogates the dispatcher itself with a fake mise. Note what is not verified: the root’s own typos.toml and lychee.toml sit outside the mirror list and have quietly diverged from their template parents (statically evident via diff), a small crack precisely at the edge of the machine’s jurisdiction. Dagger, meanwhile, is neither central nor ceremonial: five fixtures carry its wrapper, the root gate ignores it, and one named task proves one representative profile in a digest-pinned container. That is Dagger as a strategic reserve — isolation on demand, never on the critical path, and the repository’s stated reason is cost, not doubt (README.md: no hosted workflow, by choice).

C

“Defensive craftsmanship, quietly thorough” Layered fortifications clang-format = "22.1.8"

Apparent philosophy

The C profile treats undefined behavior and silent portability rot as the enemies, and answers with pinned Clang tooling, sanitizer presets, and a build script that goes further than most C projects ever do: c-build.sh synthesizes a throwaway downstream consumer project, installs the library, and compiles against it with find_package to prove the CMake package config actually works (C/c-build.sh, the run_install_check function). It even detects CompCert by executable name in C/CMakeLists.txt and softens flags for it — a formally-verified-compiler escape hatch few templates think about.

Ecosystem fit

C99, -Wall -Wextra -Wpedantic -Wconversion -Wshadow, ASan/UBSan presets, and a MinGW cross lane are all recognizably mainstream systems-C practice. The lint gate is clang-tidy delivered through clangd --check per file (C/c-quality.sh) rather than run-clang-tidy — unusual plumbing, idiomatic outcome.

Strongest qualities

The install-consumer check and the four-preset build matrix are the standout: the template does not just claim portability, it manufactures a consumer to test it. The .clang-tidy selection (C/.clang-tidy) is restrained — bugprone, cert, analyzer, portability, performance — with only bugprone and analyzer promoted to errors, which reads as someone who has actually triaged tidy noise.

Internal tensions

Two shell scripts (c-build.sh, c-quality.sh) sit outside the mise task grammar as language-owned machinery; the catalog's own principles prefer boring portable defaults, and 5.6 KB of orchestration bash is where that preference bends. The clangd-per-file loop is also the slowest possible way to run tidy checks.

Human experience

Approachable for a C developer: presets are named for what they do, and the quality script prints tool versions before judging you. The consumer check will confuse newcomers the first time it writes CMake files into build/.

Agent experience

Good: markers (CMakeLists.txt + *.c) route the dispatcher correctly, tasks are deterministic, and failures are file-specific. The dynamic dependency on a generated compile_commands.json is the one piece of hidden state.

How this standard feels

It feels like a template written by someone who has been burned by a package config that only worked in-tree. Careful, a little belt-and-suspenders, never showy.

C#

“Ecosystem-native maximalism” Toolchain purists dotnet = "10.0.301"

Apparent philosophy

The C# profile is the purest expression of “turn the platform's own dials to maximum.” One tool (dotnet), one 26-line C#/Directory.Build.props that flips AnalysisMode=AllEnabledByDefault, TreatWarningsAsErrors, nullable, deterministic builds, locked restore in CI, and NuGet audit at low severity for all transitive packages. The 309-line C#/.editorconfig then legislates style down to modifier order and parenthesization.

Ecosystem fit

Extremely idiomatic: central package management, Directory.Build.props, and .editorconfig-driven analyzers are exactly how strict .NET shops work. The only third-party additions — Meziantou and Roslynator — are the two community analyzers a .NET maintainer would name unprompted.

Strongest qualities

Coherence between documentation and executable config is near-perfect: everything the README claims (C#/README.md) is one grep away in the props file. Lint and test run Release builds so local behavior matches CI — a small choice that eliminates a classic class of “works on my machine.”

Internal tensions

var is discouraged everywhere (csharp_style_var_for_built_in_types = false), against a sizable fraction of modern C# taste; the profile openly calls explicit locals an “advisory style preference.” And AllEnabledByDefault plus warnings-as-errors means every SDK update can break the build with new diagnostics — strictness with a moving-target edge, only partly mitigated by the pinned dotnet version.

Human experience

The gate is honest but unforgiving: analyzer output is the build contract, and a first-time contributor will meet CA rules they have never heard of. The .editorconfig is long but well-sectioned and commented.

Agent experience

Very good: one toolchain, static task graph (Mise/conf.d/20-csharp.toml), lockfile-enforced restores, and diagnostics with stable codes an agent can look up and fix mechanically.

How this standard feels

Feels like Microsoft's own strict-mode documentation given a spine. Nothing clever, everything tightened.

C++

“A worldview with a build system” Layered fortifications "pkgx:llvm.org" = "22.1.8"

Apparent philosophy

C++ is the profile where the catalog stops configuring tools and starts writing philosophy. The 8.5 KB C++/README.md is a compressed style treatise — ownership semantics, parameter-passing rules, error-model selection, noexcept discipline, assertion policy — while C++/.clang-tidy enforces what it can: WarningsAsErrors: '*' across bugprone, cert, cppcoreguidelines, modernize, performance, and readability, minus a curated exclusion list.

Ecosystem fit

Deliberately C++20-only, extensions off, no dependency manager chosen — all defensible mainstream positions. The hybrid naming convention (CamelCase types, snake_case functions, trailing-underscore members) is standard-library-adjacent but still a house choice the README explicitly owns: “a deliberate readability choice, not a claim that one C++ house style is universal.”

Strongest qualities

The exclusion list is the most articulate part: dropping modernize-use-trailing-return-type, magic-number pedantry, and the pointer-arithmetic core-guidelines checks shows judgment about which rules produce systems-code noise. Sanitizer presets on by default in the debug lanes is the right default for a template.

Internal tensions

The README makes promises the tooling cannot check — concurrency documentation, failure-model consistency, [[nodiscard]] discipline — so the profile's real strictness lives partly in prose. That is an honest limitation of C++, but it means documentation and executable configuration tell overlapping, not identical, stories. And WarningsAsErrors: '*' across ~six whole check families is the single highest-friction lint posture in the catalog.

Human experience

Punishing for casual contributors: cppcoreguidelines with everything-as-errors will reject a lot of ordinary C++. Rewarding for maintainers who share the worldview — the README teaches rather than dictates.

Agent experience

Decent: the semantic check parses headers standalone so failures land near declarations, which the catalog explicitly values. But an agent must internalize prose contracts no analyzer verifies.

How this standard feels

Feels like pair-programming with a principal engineer who has strong opinions about ownership and the citations to back them. Exhausting on day one, clarifying by day thirty.

Elixir

“Ecosystem-native, quietly maximal” Ecosystem loyalists elixir = "1.20.1-otp-29"

Apparent philosophy

Elixir gets everything the BEAM community would prescribe, chained into one strictly ordered pipeline: format → compile with warnings-as-errors → Credo strict → mix xref graph --fail-above 0 banning compile-time cycles → tests → Dialyzer with six extra flags → unused-dep/hex.audit/deps.audit → docs with warnings denied → coveralls (Mise/conf.d/20-elixir.toml). Nothing here is exotic; the ambition is in running all of it, every time.

Ecosystem fit

About as idiomatic as a Mix project can be. Elixir/.credo.exs only tightens three checks (complexity 10, nesting 3, line length 120) over strict defaults; Elixir/mix.exs declares the five dependencies every serious Elixir shop already has. The README defers Phoenix/Sobelow to project overlays instead of preloading them — a genuinely ecosystem-aware restraint.

Strongest qualities

The xref cycle ban is the sharpest single rule: zero compile-connected cycles is a real architectural constraint most templates never state. Dialyzer's :underspecs/:unmatched_returns flags push past the community's usual comfort level in a way that matches the catalog's typing obsession.

Internal tensions

The gate is fully serialized through wait_for chains — seven stages that cannot overlap — so the strictest profile ordering also produces one of the slowest sequential walls. Coverage runs with no minimum threshold: measured, displayed, never enforced, which mirrors the catalog-wide pattern of reporting without gating on numbers.

Human experience

Familiar and predictable for any Elixir developer; Dialyzer's first PLT build is the usual one-time tax. Failure messages come from tools the community already knows how to read.

Agent experience

Strong: every stage is a named task with explicit ordering, lockfile presence is checked before install, and diagnostics are line-precise.

How this standard feels

Feels like the checklist a conscientious Elixir lead keeps in their head, finally written down and made mandatory.

Fortran

“Modernization as policy” Layered fortifications "conda:gfortran" = "15.2.0"

Apparent philosophy

The Fortran profile is a manifesto against legacy Fortran, enforced mechanically. Fixed-form source extensions are rejected outright by the manifest task; implicit typing and implicit externals are disabled in Fortran/fpm.toml; builds run with -std=f2018 … -Werror -pedantic -fcheck=all. Because fpm has no lockfile, the profile invents reproducibility: a custom checker (Fortran/scripts/check-fpm-manifest.py) bans wildcard versions and moving Git branches, and the one dev dependency is pinned to an immutable revision.

Ecosystem fit

Idiomatic for the modern Fortran community (fpm, fortls, FORD, test-drive) — which is itself a minority position in a language dominated by legacy code. The profile knows this and says so: “not fixed-form legacy maintenance.”

Strongest qualities

Using fortls's --debug_parser mode as a lint gate is a clever repurposing: the language server becomes a second front-end that fails close to the offending declaration. The manifest checker is 80 clean lines of stdlib-only Python — bespoke machinery, but boring bespoke machinery.

Internal tensions

Six conda-delivered tools plus Python for one language is the heaviest toolchain-per-line-of-fixture ratio in the catalog, and conda packaging is the least deterministic pin mechanism used here. The test task also carries quiet concessions — -Wno-error=unused-function -Wno-error=maybe-uninitialized — that the lint task does not, meaning tests are judged more gently than the library.

Human experience

Fine for a modern-Fortran scientist; alien to the engineer maintaining a 1985 codebase, who is explicitly not the audience. Findent's three-space indent is enforced without discussion.

Agent experience

Good task legibility, but two custom scripts and an LSP-as-linter mean an agent has more novel failure shapes to learn than in mainstream profiles.

How this standard feels

Feels like a working scientist's insistence that this time, the new project will not decay into the old ones.

GDScript

“The most engineered profile in the catalog” Custom machinists godot = "4.7-stable"

Apparent philosophy

GDScript gets treated like a systems language, which almost nobody does. GDScript/project.godot promotes untyped declarations and every unsafe dynamic access to compile errors. GDToolkit is installed from a hand-rolled, hash-pinned PEP 751 universal lock (GDScript/.config/python/pylock.gdtoolkit.toml) into a tool-owned venv. The lint gate imports the project headlessly, snapshots .uid/.import metadata with git hash-object before and after, parses every owned script with --check-only, and finally loads each script resource to catch dependency failures.

Ecosystem fit

This is not how the Godot community works — most projects use GUT or gdUnit and tolerate dynamic typing. The profile deliberately rejects test frameworks (“a small dependency-free test entrypoint”) and dynamic style. Low idiom, high conviction.

Strongest qualities

The import-metadata drift check is genuinely inventive: it turns Godot's habit of silently regenerating sidecar files into a CI failure with instructions. The lock pipeline gives a GUI game engine's linter the same reproducibility discipline as a Cargo project.

Internal tensions

Confidence is the problem. The gate's semantics depend on grepping Godot's headless output for SCRIPT ERROR|Parse Error|ERROR: — a string contract with a game engine's logger that no static reading can validate, and the profile's correctness rides on it. The machinery is also the costliest to maintain: a Godot version bump can shift warning IDs, logger formats, and importer behavior simultaneously.

Human experience

A Godot hobbyist will find this hostile; a studio shipping typed GDScript will find it visionary. There is no middle audience.

Agent experience

Surprisingly strong: the profile exists so that a non-interactive process can validate a GUI engine project deterministically. That is agent-first design in its clearest form.

How this standard feels

Feels like someone decided a game engine deserved the same rigor as a compiler toolchain, and refused to accept that the ecosystem disagreed.

Go

“A constitution with its own supreme court” House dialects go = "1.26.5"

Apparent philosophy

Go is where the catalog legislates the language itself. The profile freezes Go's expressiveness at roughly the 1.22 waterline: range-over-function iterators (Go 1.23) and generic methods (Go 1.27) are banned for project code. Enforcement is triple-locked: the go 1.26 language directive in Go/go.mod makes generic methods a compile error today; depguard bans iter imports in Go/.golangci.yml; and a custom analyzer, boringlint, structurally rejects iterator-shaped types, range-over-func, and generic-method declarations and selections on dependencies (Go/boringlint/boringlint.go).

Ecosystem fit

The style layer is hyper-idiomatic — gofumpt, go mod tidy -diff, go vet, govulncheck, a curated 30-linter golangci set. The dialect layer is anti-idiomatic by definition: it rejects features the ecosystem has adopted, including the standard library's own iter package. The profile knows the difference and argues for it in Go/README.md: “here, elegance means directness.”

Strongest qualities

boringlint is 235 lines of careful go/types work — it chases type-parameter constraints through unions and embedded interfaces to catch iterator shapes hidden behind generics, and its testdata proves the edge cases (Go/boringlint/testdata/src/rangefunc/rangefunc.go). A //go:build go1.27 test file pre-verifies behavior on a toolchain that does not exist for this module yet. As custom machinery goes, it is unusually well-evidenced.

Internal tensions

The analyzer is the profile's own contradiction: a catalog that preaches “boring, dependable machinery over clever local systems” ships its most sophisticated program to enforce boringness. It is a nested module with its own go.sum, a cached vet binary under .cache/, and a permanent obligation to track go/types across releases. The redundancy is at least self-aware — three mechanisms enforcing one policy reads as an intentional boundary, and the README frames it that way.

Human experience

Day-to-day Go feels normal until you touch the fence: write a func(yield func(int) bool) and the tool explains, in a full sentence, what to do instead. The error messages are the best-written in the catalog.

Agent experience

Exceptional. The dialect exists partly for agents: a smaller language with visible data flow is easier to reason about, and the analyzer converts a prose preference into a deterministic failure an agent can respond to.

How this standard feels

Feels like a shop that watched Go grow features and decided, in writing, which Go it wanted to keep. You may disagree with the verdict; you cannot call it careless.

Haskell

“Restraint, by Haskell standards” Ecosystem loyalists ghcup = "0.2.6.2"

Apparent philosophy

08

Productive tensions

Strictness—VS—Approachabilityresolved at the second pole's expense

The catalog resolves this trade almost always in strictness’s favor, then compensates with prose. Python’s reportAny = "error" (Python/pyproject.toml), TypeScript’s all-false strict-boolean-expressions, SPARK’s level-4 proof gate — each is a wall a newcomer hits on day one. The compensation is real: every README opens with permission to relax (a strict seed, not a finished architecture), and error messages ship their own remediation. But permission-to-relax is documentation, and the walls are executable; the asymmetry tells you which one wins.

Uniformity—VS—Ecosystem idiomheld in balance

The six-verb grammar is uniformity done right — it standardizes names, not meanings. Under lint, Elixir runs Credo and cycle checks, Zig just compiles twice, SPARK runs a validation build; each fragment is shaped in its ecosystem’s native material (Mise/conf.d/). Uniformity turns artificial exactly twice, where the catalog legislates against its ecosystem rather than with it: Go’s iterator ban and TypeScript’s language-region bans. Both are argued openly, which is the difference between a house dialect and an accident.

Explicit policy—VS—Redundant configurationresolved: first pole wins

Go enforces one rule three ways — language directive, depguard, custom analyzer (Go/README.md) — and the README frames the overlap as intentional: the directive enforces today, the analyzer survives toolchain drift, depguard catches the import path. Elsewhere redundancy is less deliberate: PHP’s three tools litigate the same type hints with different case law, and the root’s Markdown configs drifted from their template parents precisely where the mirror list stops (typos.toml vs Markdown/typos.toml). Redundancy communicates a boundary when it is symmetric and stated; it becomes residue when it accumulates silently.

Self-containment—VS—Tool countresolved: first pole wins

The covenant is that mise pins everything and PATH supplies nothing. Sixteen profiles keep it. Lua breaks it for luarocks and PHP for Composer — both with honest error messages (Mise/conf.d/20-lua.toml, 20-php.toml), both because pinning a package manager’s package manager is where the recursion gets absurd. Fortran strains it differently: six conda-delivered tools is self-contained in letter and heavyweight in spirit. The covenant holds where it matters; the exceptions are the map of where the ecosystems themselves are unfinished.

Human readability—VS—Agent determinismresolved at the second pole's expense

Mostly these reinforce each other here — deterministic commands are also learnable commands. They diverge in the proof layer: byte-for-byte mirrors, per-platform lockfiles, and digest-pinned containers are built for machine verification, and a human maintaining them navigates rules (which files are mirrored? which fixture configs are “full” vs “minimal”?) that only the drift checker fully knows (scripts/check-standards-drift.py, check_fixture_config). The catalog’s answer is to make the checker’s error messages the documentation — workable, but it means the deepest rules live in Python, not prose.

Local simplicity—VS—Repository-wide machineryheld in balance

Each fixture is deliberately tiny — a greeter, a counter, a doubler — and each language fragment is readable in one sitting. The simplicity is purchased by the root: a monorepo scheduler bounded to two jobs, environment sanitization for Go (env -u GOROOT -u GOTOOLDIR), a nested child mise process documented as a validator workaround (.config/mise/config.toml), and the drift checker’s 613 lines. This is the catalog’s honest ledger: the complexity did not disappear, it was relocated to one place, tested, and commented with its expiry conditions.

Portable templates—VS—Repository-specific proofresolved at the second pole's expense

Fixtures prove one thing precisely: the copied layout works on pinned Linux tools through mise. They do not prove macOS, Windows, other CI systems, or real project shapes — and the docs say so (testers/README.md). Two profiles reach beyond that boundary on their own: C and C++ manufacture a synthetic downstream consumer to prove their install trees (C/c-build.sh), the strongest portability evidence in the repository. The Dagger lane could generalize that discipline but is wired to a single profile — proof exists; coverage is a choice still half-made.

09

Unusual choices worth discussing

The drift checker contains a test harness statically evident

scripts/check-standards-drift.py does not just diff files; it writes a fake mise executable to a temp dir, fabricates marker files for all eighteen profiles plus four negative cases, executes the real dispatcher script against them, and asserts the exact dispatch log. A lint script with an embedded integration-test rig is the clearest single artifact of this repository’s character: verification is never left to convention.

Go bans features the language shipped statically evident

Range-over-function iterators and generic methods are rejected by boringlint, with a //go:build go1.27 test pre-verifying behavior on a toolchain the module does not yet use (Go/boringlint/genericmethod_go127_test.go). Enforcing a frozen dialect reveals the repository’s deepest belief: that a language is a liability curve, and you may choose your point on it.

A game engine gets a cryptographic lockfile statically evident

GDScript’s linter toolchain is resolved into a universal, hash-pinned PEP 751 lock and regenerated-and-compared on every gate (Mise/conf.d/20-godot.toml, godot:lock:check). Nobody in the Godot world asks for this; the catalog does it anyway because the covenant is catalog-wide, not per-ecosystem.

awk as a TOML parser, twice statically evident

SPARK’s exact-pin policy and lock verification are implemented as ~100 lines of awk regex over alire.toml and alire.lock (Mise/conf.d/20-spark.toml). It works, it is self-contained, and it is the point where “boring machinery” and “robust machinery” visibly part ways — the typo checker had to be told to look away (typos.toml).

Machine-suggested refactors are mandatory statically evident

PHP’s CI gate includes rector process --dry-run (PHP/composer.json): if the modernization tool would change your code, the build fails. This inverts the usual relationship with automated refactoring — Rector’s opinion is not advice, it is law, and code style can now change because a tool released a new rule.

A check that cannot fail statically evident

Python’s deep tier runs interrogate --fail-under=0 (Mise/conf.d/20-python.toml) — docstring coverage measured, printed, and never enforced. In a catalog where nearly every number is a gate, one deliberate piece of pure telemetry stands out; it reads as a considered refusal to gate on a coverage metric, consistent with do not chase coverage numbers for their own sake (AGENTS.md).

The GitHub language graph is curated statically evident

Root .gitattributes marks testers/** and maintenance scripts linguist-detectable=false so fixtures don’t dominate the repository’s language statistics. Cosmetic — and telling: the maintainer thinks about how the catalog reads from the outside, the same instinct behind the essay-quality READMEs.

Alpha tooling inside the paranoid profile statically evident

Kotlin pairs mandatory sha256 dependency verification with Detekt at 2.0.0-alpha.5 and allRules = true (Kotlin/build.gradle.kts). Supply-chain rigor and unstable-analyzer appetite in one profile is the catalog’s strangest risk allocation — unless the position is that integrity threats and lint churn are simply different categories of risk, which the config never says aloud.

PHPStan runs with proc_open disabled statically evident

The stan script invokes php -d disable_functions=proc_open (PHP/composer.json) — hardening the analyzer’s own runtime against subprocess spawning. A tiny choice that shows the threat model extends to the quality tools themselves.

2,226 lines of personal dotfiles ride along statically evident

extras/workstation/ holds two large, careful bootstrap scripts (Ghostty, tmux, LazyVim, Homebrew/apt) — explicitly excepted from the mise-only rule because they install mise itself. They are the least catalog-like thing here: personal, host-mutating, convergent-by-design. Their presence says this is one person’s (or one small team’s) complete engineering environment, not an abstract standard.

Closing essay

Is it coherent? Remarkably, yes — and the coherence is load-bearing rather than cosmetic. The same convictions recur at every altitude: pin it, prove it, prefer the type system, write the error message like you’ll be the one reading it. When a profile deviates (Haskell’s optional freeze, Lua’s PATH dependency), the deviation is documented in the same breath. Even the contradictions are coherent: every piece of clever machinery the catalog builds against its own “boring” principle arrives with tests, comments, and an expiry note.

Is it elegant? By its own definition of elegance — directness, obviousness, absence of ceremony — mostly. The six-verb grammar is genuinely elegant. The fixtures are elegant. The Rust and Zig profiles are elegant. The proof layer is not elegant; it is thorough, which the catalog occasionally mistakes for the same thing. A 613-line checker and awk TOML parsers are the price of the byte-for-byte mirror guarantee, and the repository pays it without pretending otherwise.

Is it too guarded? Against the world outside the repository, sometimes: PHP’s seven walls, Kotlin’s checksum ritual, and the universal warnings-as-errors posture assume adversarial entropy everywhere. Against itself, no — and that is the more interesting fact. The catalog audits its own copies, lints its own prose, tests its own lint scripts, and curates its own appearance. Its deepest fear is not attackers; it is silent divergence — the copy that stops matching, the doc that stops being true, the version that moved while nobody watched.

Does it understand the ecosystems it governs? Deeply, and the evidence is in the exceptions: -Werror scoped so Haskell packages stay publishable, Rust’s cast lints relaxed with a systems-code rationale, Phoenix and Sobelow deferred to overlays, fixed-form Fortran ruled out-of-scope rather than pretended-supported. Where it overrides an ecosystem — Go’s dialect, TypeScript’s bans, GDScript’s typing regime — it does so knowingly, in writing, with an argument you can disagree with.

Are the intended workflows discoverable? This is the catalog’s cleanest win. mise run tasks from any directory answers “what can I do here”; every gate names its remediation; the manifest is the single map from template to fixture to task fragment. A stranger — carbon or silicon — can find the workflow without asking anyone, which is exactly the design target the repository set for itself.

Who thrives here, and who resists? Thriving looks like: small teams shipping long-lived systems software, security-adjacent shops, solo maintainers who want their standards to outlive their attention, and — explicitly — autonomous agents, for whom this may be the most hospitable polyglot environment I have reviewed. Resisting looks like: product teams optimizing iteration speed, researchers and prototypers, Godot hobbyists, PHP application developers with living codebases, and anyone whose relationship to a language includes the features this catalog voted off the island. The catalog would not dispute the list; its READMEs all but publish it.

The thesis, then. Most standards repositories are lists of preferences waiting to erode. This one is an argument that taste can be made durable — that if you pin every version, mirror every copy, test every checker, and write down every reason, an engineering culture can survive its own author’s absence and even the arrival of contributors who are not human. The machinery occasionally betrays the modesty it preaches, the strictness occasionally outruns the evidence for it, and two profiles quietly borrow tools from the host machine like everyone else. But as a piece of engineering culture expressed in executable form, it achieves something rare: you could disagree with every one of its opinions and still trust every one of its claims — because the repository was built so the claims, unlike the opinions, are checked by a machine.
